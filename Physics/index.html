Overview

Charlie started his Physics journey beginning with an undergraduate degree in physics and a diploma in applied mathematics from the University of Melbourne.

After completing these degrees concurrently at the end of 2018, he started a year working in big data management for a technology consulting company, Capgemini.

However, the ongoing teaching Charlie was doing during this period was a stark reminder of his passion and he returned to study his masters at Melbourne University as the state settled into lockdown in June of 2020.

He enjoyed getting stuck into further coursework receiving high marks in subjects such as condensed matter physics and quantum field theory while completing a research thesis under the supervision of Liam Hall, David Simpson, Nikolai Dontschuk and Daniel McCloskey.

The research explored methods to modify the surface of diamond to make a more effective sensor for measuring electrical activity of cells in vitro.

Some high level discussions around the sensor, termed as a diamond voltage imaging microscope, can be found here in the University of Melbourne Pursuit magazine and in this Nature research highlight.

If you're curious for more of a high level discussion feel free to check out the science communicaton and education section below.

For the more technically minded, Charlie worked on optimizing the Diamond Voltage Imaging Microscope by experimenting with electrochemical oxidation methods to modify the surface of a diamond substrates to achieve bespoke concentrations of hydrogen and oxygen groups on the surface.

The study monitored the effectiveness of successive electrochemical oxidations by measuring and characterising the fluorescence of nitrogen vacancy centres present in the positive (unoxidised surface), neutral and negative charge states (oxidised surface).

Not only were the nitrogen vacancy centres reporters of the oxidaton effectiveness but at specific surface mixtures (only partially oxidised) they became highly sensitive sensors for measuring local electrical activity. Hence the Diamond Voltage Imaging Microscope, or DVIM for short.

The results of this research directly contributed to the previously linked article demonstrating the DVIM in nanophotonics.

Charlie is now undertaking his PhD at the University of Melbourne once again under the supervision of David Simpson, Nikolai Dontschuk and Daniel McCloskey looking at further improvements of the DVIM and applications to biological systems such as organoids (lab grown organs).
